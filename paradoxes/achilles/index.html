Achilles and the Tortoise

Zeno of Elea, c. 450 BCE — an argument that motion is impossible, which took mathematics two millennia to answer, and philosophy is arguing about still.

1 · The paradox

Achilles, the fastest of the Greeks, races a tortoise. Being a good sport, he grants it a head start. Zeno argues that Achilles can never overtake it:

“In a race, the quickest runner can never overtake the slowest, since the pursuer must first reach the point whence the pursued started, so that the slower must always hold a lead.”
— Zeno's argument, as reported by Aristotle, Physics VI:9, 239b14

Before Achilles catches the tortoise, he must reach the tortoise's starting point. By the time he gets there, the tortoise has crawled a little further. Achilles must now reach that point — but meanwhile the tortoise has moved again. However fast Achilles runs, whenever he arrives where the tortoise was, the tortoise is already somewhere it wasn't. The chase decomposes into infinitely many stages, and at the end of every single one of them, the tortoise is still ahead.

Of course, real runners overtake real tortoises all the time. That is exactly what makes this a paradox: a seemingly airtight argument for a conclusion we know to be false. The question is not whether Achilles wins — it's what, precisely, is wrong with the argument.

After ? steps, the simulation reports a gap of exactly 0 — Achilles has “caught” the tortoise. But don't be fooled: the computer did not complete infinitely many steps. A JavaScript number holds about 16 significant decimal digits; once the gap became smaller than that precision, adding it to the tortoise's position changed nothing, and the gap rounded away.

This is worth pausing on. We set out to simulate an infinite process, and what we got is a finite machine quietly cutting the process short. No computer — no physical procedure of any kind that takes a minimum time per step — can literally run through infinitely many steps one at a time. In a sense, the failure of this simulation is the most honest thing it shows: it is exactly Zeno's challenge, materialized in silicon. Keep this in mind for section 4.

Try this: keep clicking Zeno step (or ×10) and watch the gap counter. It shrinks by the same factor every time and never reaches zero — until, suddenly, it does. Something happens around step 15–50 (depending on the speed ratio) that Zeno did not predict. Then read on.

3 · What mathematics can say

Let the tortoise start \(H\) meters ahead, and let Achilles run \(k\) times faster (in the default race, \(H = 100\) and \(k = 10\)). In Zeno's first stage Achilles covers the head start, \(H\). Meanwhile the tortoise, being \(k\) times slower, advances \(H/k\). In the second stage Achilles covers that \(H/k\) while the tortoise gains \(H/k^2\), and so on. The total distance Achilles runs across all of Zeno's stages is

$$ S \;=\; H + \frac{H}{k} + \frac{H}{k^2} + \frac{H}{k^3} + \cdots \;=\; \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{H}{k^n}. $$

This is a geometric series with ratio \(r = 1/k < 1\). Its partial sums have a closed form — multiply by \(r\), subtract, and everything telescopes:

$$ S_N \;=\; H\,\frac{1 - r^{N+1}}{1 - r} \;\xrightarrow{\;N \to \infty\;}\; \frac{H}{1-r} \;=\; H\,\frac{k}{k-1}. $$

For the default race: \(S = 100 \cdot \tfrac{10}{9} = 111.\overline{1}\) meters — the golden dashed line in the simulation. The same argument applies to time: each stage takes \(1/k\) as long as the one before, so the infinitely many stages consume a total of \(\tfrac{100}{10}\cdot\tfrac{10}{9} = 11.\overline{1}\) seconds. (Sanity check without any series: Achilles closes the 100 m gap at 9 m/s, which takes \(100/9 = 11.\overline{1}\) s. The two computations agree.)

This is the decisive mathematical insight, and it was genuinely unavailable to Zeno's contemporaries: infinitely many positive quantities can add up to a finite amount. Zeno's stages are infinite in number, but they shrink fast enough that they fit inside 111.11 meters and 11.11 seconds. The argument's hidden premise — that an infinity of stages must take forever — is simply false. The rigorous theory of convergence (Cauchy and Weierstrass, 19th century) makes this precise: the partial sums \(S_N\) come arbitrarily close to \(111.\overline{1}\), and the “sum” of the series is defined as that limit.

Notice what the mathematics delivers: it tells us exactly where and when Achilles draws level, and it dissolves the claim that infinitely many stages need infinite time. What it does not obviously do is explain how the stages get completed. That's the next section.

4 · So is the paradox resolved?

It is tempting to stop here and declare victory: the series converges, therefore Zeno was wrong, case closed. Many textbooks do exactly that. But look more carefully at what each side is claiming.

The convergence argument answers the question “where and when would Achilles catch the tortoise?” with beautiful precision. Zeno, however, was asking a different question: “how can a sequence of acts that has no last member ever be finished?” Summing the series presupposes that all the stages get completed and computes where their completion happens. It doesn't explain what it even means to perform infinitely many acts one after another — what philosophers now call a supertask. Assigning a finite limit to the series and explaining the completability of the run are not the same thing.

Aristotle's response was to distinguish two kinds of infinity: the runner's path is potentially infinitely divisible — you can mark as many division points as you like — but the runner never confronts an actually completed infinity of parts; he just runs. On this view Zeno's infinite decomposition is something we describe onto the motion, not something the motion has to do. Modern discussions push further: Max Black argued that completing infinitely many distinct acts is logically incoherent; James Thomson invented his famous lamp (switched infinitely often in one minute — is it on or off at the end?) to sharpen the puzzle; Paul Benacerraf and Adolf Grünbaum replied that such supertasks may be perfectly consistent, just underdetermined. The debate is genuinely still open — not about whether Achilles wins, but about what his winning tells us regarding infinity, continuity, and whether spacetime itself is infinitely divisible.

And here our simulation earns its keep — precisely by failing. If you pushed the race far enough, you saw the computer round the gap away after a few dozen steps: a finite machine cannot execute Zeno's stages one by one, so it cheats. Every step-by-step process with a minimum cost per step faces the same wall; the only reason the real Achilles finishes is that motion, whatever it is, is not a step-by-step process. Contrast the two buttons in the simulation: Zeno step, pressed forever, never completes the race — Run the real race completes it in eleven unremarkable seconds. The whole paradox lives in the space between those two buttons.

That, more than any formula, is the milestone this paradox represents: Zeno forced mathematics to eventually build a rigorous theory of limits and the continuum — and even equipped with that theory, we are still arguing about what infinity has to do with the world. A recurring theme of this course, first spotted by a philosopher watching an imaginary footrace 2,500 years ago.
