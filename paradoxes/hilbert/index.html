Hilbert's Hotel

David Hilbert, Göttingen, 1924 — a hotel where “every room is occupied” and “we have room for you” are both true, and what finally overbooks it.

1 · The story

Imagine a hotel with infinitely many rooms, numbered 1, 2, 3, … — one room for every natural number, with no last room. Tonight the hotel is completely full: every single room has a guest in it. A traveler arrives at the front desk and asks for a room.

In any ordinary hotel, the story ends there. But the manager of the Grand Hotel does not apologize. She switches on the intercom and makes one announcement: “Dear guests, would everyone kindly move to the room whose number is one higher than your current room.” The guest in room 1 moves to room 2, the guest in room 2 to room 3, and so on — every guest still has a room, because there is no last guest who falls off the end. Room 1 is now empty, and the traveler checks in. A full hotel just accommodated one more guest, and it is still full.

The thought experiment comes from a 1924 Göttingen lecture by David Hilbert, and reached the wider world through George Gamow's One Two Three… Infinity (1947). Unlike Zeno's race, nothing here is an illusion or a trick to be debunked: every step of the story is mathematically sound. The paradox is only that it collides head-on with intuitions we built in a world of finite hotels. Try it yourself below — including the one bus that even this hotel cannot handle.

The computer just ran out of numbers.

Somewhere around room 9,007,199,254,740,992 (that is 253), something strange happened to the doors: two neighboring rooms started displaying the same number. The hotel didn't run out of rooms — the computer ran out of numbers. JavaScript stores numbers in 64-bit floating point, which can only tell integers apart up to 253; beyond that, n + 1 === n.

The same lesson as the Achilles page, from the opposite direction: there, the machine could not make its steps small enough; here, it cannot count high enough. The hotel's infinity was never in the machine — it lives in the rule “room \(n \mapsto n+1\)”, which needs no counting at all.

3 · What mathematics can say

The hotel runs on one idea: two sets have the same size if their members can be paired off perfectly — a one-to-one correspondence, or bijection. For finite sets this agrees with counting. For infinite sets, it is the only definition we have — and it produces the hotel's magic. The intercom announcement

$$ f(n) = n + 1 $$

pairs the rooms \(\{1, 2, 3, \ldots\}\) perfectly with the rooms \(\{2, 3, 4, \ldots\}\): nobody is left without a room, nobody has to share, and room 1 falls out of the pairing — free for the newcomer. The bus works the same way with

$$ g(n) = 2n, $$

which pairs all the current guests with just the even rooms, leaving the infinitely many odd rooms for the infinitely many passengers: passenger \(k\) takes room \(2k - 1\). Two infinite crowds, one full hotel, everybody sleeps. In fact this property is so characteristic that Dedekind used it as the definition: a set is infinite precisely when it can be paired off with a proper part of itself. What looks like a bug of infinity is its defining feature.

Sets that can be paired with \(\mathbb{N} = \{1, 2, 3, \ldots\}\) are called countable, and the hotel is their playground: it absorbs one newcomer, a busload, even countably many buses. So is every infinity countable? In 1891 Georg Cantor showed the answer is no — and the hotel meets its match. Suppose the front desk proposes any room list for the passengers of the Cantor bus, whose seats are the real numbers in \([0,1]\):

$$ \text{room } 1 \to 0.d_{11}d_{12}d_{13}\ldots, \quad \text{room } 2 \to 0.d_{21}d_{22}d_{23}\ldots, \quad \text{room } 3 \to 0.d_{31}d_{32}d_{33}\ldots, \;\ldots $$

Build a stowaway number \(\rho\) by walking down the diagonal and changing every digit: its \(n\)-th digit differs from \(d_{nn}\). Then \(\rho\) disagrees with the guest of room \(n\) at the \(n\)-th decimal place — for every \(n\). The stowaway is on the bus but on nobody's list, no matter what list the desk tries. The reals are strictly more numerous than the naturals: infinity comes in sizes, \( \aleph_0 < \mathfrak{c} \), and Cantor's tower of infinities \( \aleph_0, \aleph_1, \aleph_2, \ldots \) rises from exactly this argument.

4 · What does it mean?

Zeno's race and Hilbert's hotel are opposite kinds of paradox. Zeno argues for a conclusion everyone knows is false (Achilles never wins), so something in the argument must give. The hotel's conclusions are true — provably so — and what gives is our intuition. Philosophers call this a veridical paradox: the shock is the evidence that our everyday notion of “size” and “full” carries a hidden assumption of finiteness. Drop the assumption, and “full but accepting guests” is no more contradictory than “moved one room over.”

Notice also how the hotel handles infinity — because it is the exact opposite of Zeno's runner. Achilles had to pass through his stages one after another, and no step-by-step process finishes infinitely many acts; our Achilles simulation duly collapsed after seventeen clicks. The hotel manager never visits a single room. She states a rule — \(n \mapsto n+1\) — and infinitely many guests move in one act. This is precisely the move modern mathematics made after Cantor: infinity became manageable not by enumerating it, but by reasoning about functions and correspondences as finished wholes. (Whether such actual infinities exist anywhere outside mathematics — whether any real corridor could hold them — is a question Hilbert's contemporaries fought over, and physics still cannot answer.)

Our simulation is honest about the same limit as always: your screen only ever shows finitely many rooms, and if you hunt for the corridor's end, the machine gives out at \(2^{53}\) before the hotel gives out at all. The infinity is not in the pixels; it is in the rule. That a finite mind — or machine — can still reason reliably about it is, as Hilbert put it, the mathematician's paradise, and no one was going to expel us from it.
